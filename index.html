A Dynamic Concept of Social Work Personnel and Pediatric Asthma Triggers:

Indoor Air Quality and Environmental Trigger Management

Southeast Missouri State University

Concept

Environmental conditions have a tremendous impact on the health and welfare of pediatric asthma patients. Social work's contribution to the protection of child health by assessing indoor environmental asthma triggers is a challenging but crucial activity.

Goal: To provide patient caregivers with the information necessary to raise awareness of the indoor asthma triggers that may be present in their homes and increase confidence in managing their child’s asthma.

ENVIRONMENTAL TRIGGER EDUCATION FOR PATIENTS/CAREGIVERS
What are the indoor environmental triggers that affect asthma symptom onset?
  • Tobacco Smoke
  • Pests (cockroaches, mice)
  • Mold/Fungi
  • Pet Dander
  • Dust Mites
  • Endotoxins
Are there control measures for these triggers?

Clean the home to reduce the number of dust mites and pet dander contained in pillows, carpets, stuffed animals, and bedding. Cover bedding and pillows with sealable, anti-allergenic plastic covers when possible.

Clean areas with visible mold/fungi and stop any water leaks inside the living area.

Clean air cleaning filters present in heating, ventilating, and air conditioning systems on a regular basis.

Clean food storage areas; keep foods in sealable containers, and empty trash and garbage to minimize pest impacts in the home.

Keep windows closed during allergy season.

Do not let family pets sleep with those affected by asthma. Keep the pet outside when possible.

Do not smoke in the home or car.

QUESTIONS TO ASK THE PATIENT OR PATIENT FAMILY MEMBERS

Do you understand how environmental triggers affect the onset of asthma in your child?

How much do you think it will help your child to reduce or remove these triggers from your home?

How much do you think it will help you to recognize the first signs of asthma symptom onset?

Will you be able to provide the medications necessary for your child during mild or severe attacks?

Do you have access to rescue treatments when necessary?

Do you understand the medical directions about asthma and asthma treatment provided by your health care provider?

Do you have access and transportation to medical appointments?

Do you have an asthma action plan? Does your child’s school nurse have this plan? Have you discussed your child’s asthma plan with the nurse?

HOME ASSESSMENT OBSERVATIONS

What is going on in the child’s life at home? Are there stressors, environmental, cultural and/or psychosocial, affecting the child?

Is there obvious mold growth in the home? Where?

Is there a pet living in the home? Does it sleep with the child?

Is the home in good repair? Are there signs of water leaks?

Are there any signs of pests (cockroaches, rodents) in the home? Is refuse being disposed of properly? Are foods in containers sealed? Are good housekeeping practices being followed in the kitchen?

Are there visible signs of dust in the air or on flat surfaces? Are there smokers in the home?
